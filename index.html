Media and Information Literacy (MIL) – Lesson 5

This lesson explores key sourcing methods in Media and Information Literacy, focusing on credible, ethical, and culturally relevant information sources.

Sourcing Indigenous Media

Indigenous media refers to knowledge, information, and communication practices that originate from native or local communities. Much of this knowledge is passed down orally and reflects a community’s culture, values, and traditions.

  • Highly credible due to proximity to the source and non-profit circulation.
  • Serves as a channel for education, change, and development in rural communities.
  • In the Philippines, it promotes cultural pride and preserves local knowledge.

Sourcing in Libraries and Print Media

A library is an institution that houses books, manuscripts, recordings, films, and other reference materials for public use and research.

  • Libraries organize information for effective research and evaluation.
  • Librarians teach criteria such as authority, accuracy, and relevance.
  • Enhances understanding of media techniques and their societal impact.

Sourcing on the Internet and Online Resources

The internet is a global network that provides access to vast amounts of information through interconnected digital platforms and databases.

  • Verify credibility by checking authors, publishers, and publication dates.
  • Use effective keywords and cross-check information across multiple sources.
  • Apply critical thinking when using websites, databases, and digital libraries.

Sourcing from Other Platforms

Other information platforms include community media, newspapers, magazines, social media, and encyclopedias, each offering different types of content.

  • Evaluate sources based on accuracy, authority, value, and timeliness.
  • Organizations like UNESCO promote digital and media literacy initiatives.
  • Encourages intercultural dialogue and responsible media use.
